Lake Fishing in Arizona

Reservoirs and urban lakes give anglers room to cast from shore, wade shallow flats, or fish from a boat or float tube. Lake levels swing with snowmelt and municipal demand, so ramps and weed lines shift from year to year. Bring polarized sunglasses to spot structure and watch for windy afternoons that make casting a challenge.

Lake Powell

How to get there: Lake Powell spans northern Arizona and southern Utah. Most anglers launch from marinas near Page, Arizona, after checking highway conditions across the Colorado Plateau. Houseboat and powerboat traffic is heavy in summer—stay visible and wear a life jacket.

Best time of year: Spring and fall often bring stable weather and active bass and stripers. Winter can be quiet but rewarding for patient anglers; summer is busy and hot.

Flies to try: Clouser minnows, deceivers, and crayfish patterns fished on sinking or intermediate lines along cliffs and drop-offs. Poppers and gurglers early and late in the day.

Fish you may find: Largemouth and smallmouth bass, striped bass, walleye, and catfish depending on depth and forage.

Roosevelt Lake

How to get there: Roosevelt lies northeast of the Phoenix metro area; AZ 188 runs along much of the eastern shore with developed ramps and camping. Confirm which ramps are open before you haul a boat.

Best time of year: Spring spawning periods can concentrate bass shallow; winter fronts may push fish deeper. Watch for algae blooms in warm months and adjust where you fish.

Flies to try: Woolly buggers in olive and black, crawdad patterns, and baitfish streamers. For panfish near cover, try small poppers and foam spiders.

Fish you may find: Largemouth bass, crappie, catfish, and sunfish are common targets for fly anglers.

Tempe Town Lake

How to get there: Tempe Town Lake sits near downtown Tempe with shoreline parks and pedestrian bridges. Parking is available in city lots; read posted rules for fishing hours and special events that may close areas.

Best time of year: Mild winter days and spring mornings are popular. Summer afternoons can be windy— shorter leaders and heavier flies help.

Flies to try: Damselfly and dragonfly nymphs, leech patterns, and small baitfish streamers. Bright attractors can draw strikes when water is stained.

Fish you may find: Stocked trout when the water is cool; bass, carp, and catfish in warmer conditions.
